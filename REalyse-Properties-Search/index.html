REalyse Properties Search

Modifying the experience and interface of data analytics company property search page to fix problems coming from the backend and improve the overall user experience.

Overview

REalyse is a real estate data and analytics platform that helps UK residential property professionals make informed decisions. By aggregating property data, they help professionals save time and effort. However, while the quantity of data on the platform is one of their greatest selling points, it can also present a challenge when trying to present it in a clear, coherent way. The ‘properties’ page, where users could search for current and historical property data on a map, was one such area that needed some help.

The Problem

When selecting properties on the map, the pop-up would be placed overtop the property and caused many problems to users. When they selected properties on certain parts of the screen, the pop-up would often be cropped out of the screen, would move erratically or disappear as the user dragged to different parts of the screen.

It was also not an ideal way to display information, especially for multiple properties in one place. People would have to scroll through the list of properties within the pop-up which was very narrow. Also, there was no clear hierarchy of information within the pop-up.

  1. Pop-ups moving erratically around the screen
  2. Pop-ups getting cropped out of the screen
  3. Due to the quantity of properties in an area, too many results all showing on the map, many overtop each other which caused problems in the backend and how it appeared on the screen (dark shadows, empty UI elements)
  4. As the pop-ups appeared by the location of the property, they would often conceal other results which could be frustrating for users looking for properties in a very specific area
  5. Multiple properties on one pop-up would have users scrolling through the results

Process

I started this process having a chat with the developer and another designer in the team, in order to fully understand the problem and identify any constraints. Then I jumped straight into my user research. I was able to do a call with a user where he shared his screen and I asked him to complete specific tasks. Another form of user research I did was through Fullstory. It was a great opportunity to watch how a wide variety of users experienced searching for properties. There were a few clear takeaways:

  1. Crowding of properties was really frustrating for users as they wouldn’t be able to access certain results in high-density areas on the map
  2. Arriving at a property took too much time and clicks
  3. Users couldn’t see properties on certain parts of their screen as the pop-up would be cropped out

Now that I had a much greater understanding of the problem and how the users were experiencing the Properties section, I began doing some preliminary sketches, getting some initial ideas on paper. I kept these greyscale and didn’t focus too much on the details. One particular idea for the pop-up really stood out as a solution so I took it onto Sketch to explore it further.

For that map, I explored several different ideas and functionalities in my sketches. One thing that frustrated me about the current journey, was that users had to click either on the zoom-in button or double-clicking on the map to arrive at the properties they wanted to view. The UI elements weren’t clickable when they were zoomed-out, users were able to view the pop-ups once the clusters broke apart into smaller groups.

I thought the elements should serve a greater purpose, so I spoke to the developers about different functionality. When users select a cluster of properties when zoomed out, they would be automatically zoomed in a level so that the cluster would break apart. There would be a few zoom levels, with properties staying clustered longer so that we wouldn’t have the problem of too many properties appearing at once, all on top of each other, causing elements to break. There would a range around each property that would determine whether a property remained in a cluster even when zoomed all the way. This way, there would be no overlapping elements, no odd shadows, no missing or blank elements.

After receiving the green light from developers about this functionality, I mocked up the user flow and created a quick prototype on Invision. Using this prototype, I tested the idea with several people in the room from outside the product team. Watching several people interact with the prototype, I was able to see what was working and what needed improvement.

Handoff and Final Product

I continued to iterate on this until I was happy with the results and ready to show it around again. Following successful testing, I had a meeting with the developers who would be working with the designs in order to handoff and go through the functionality.

Learnings

1. Simplicity is best

I continued to iterate on this until I was happy with the results and ready to show it around again. Following successful testing, I had a meeting with the developers who would be working with the designs in order to handoff and go through the functionality.

2. Collaboration for the win

Because I worked closely and communicated with the developers throughout my process, production went much more smoothly. There was a greater understanding of the desired functionality and our idea for the final product was aligned. However, even with close communication, unexpected issues can arise. The UI element I designed for the zoomed-out view (different to the element which would indicate users can click for the pop-up) was not implemented in this release. It was done the next release and it was pushed live anyhow, as the changes were still clear without it.
